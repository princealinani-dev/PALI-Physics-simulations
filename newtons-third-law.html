📖 Definition & Core Concept
What is Newton's Third Law?

Newton's Third Law of Motion describes the relationship between action and reaction forces.

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.
or
F₁ = -F₂
If object A exerts a force on object B, then object B exerts an equal and opposite force on object A.

This law states that forces always come in pairs. When one object exerts a force on another object, the second object exerts a force back on the first object. These two forces are always equal in magnitude and opposite in direction. Importantly, action and reaction forces act on different objects.

🔄 Action & Reaction Pairs
What Makes an Action-Reaction Pair?
Characteristics of Force Pairs:
  • Equal magnitude: Both forces have the same strength
  • Opposite direction: Forces point in opposite directions
  • Same type: Both are the same type of force (both contact, both gravitational, etc.)
  • Act on different objects: Each force acts on a different object
  • Occur simultaneously: Both forces exist at the same time
Example: You Push a Wall

Action: You push the wall with 100 N to the right.
Reaction: The wall pushes you with 100 N to the left.
Result: You feel pushed back (you don't go through the wall).

Action Force
Applied BY object A, TO object B
→ Direction: Right
Reaction Force
Applied BY object B, TO object A
← Direction: Left
⚠️ Common Misconceptions
Myth 1: Action and Reaction Cancel Out

FALSE! Action and reaction forces act on different objects, so they do NOT cancel out. They cannot be added together to find a net force.

✓ Correct: Action and reaction are always in equilibrium separately for their respective objects.

Myth 2: One Object "Wins"

FALSE! Neither object "overpowers" the other. The forces are ALWAYS equal.

Whether an object accelerates depends on the net force on that object (considering ALL forces acting on it, not just the reaction force).

Myth 3: Heavy Objects Don't Feel Reaction Forces

FALSE! ALL objects, regardless of mass, experience reaction forces.

A heavy object experiences the same reaction force as a light object—it just doesn't accelerate as much due to its greater mass (Newton's Second Law: a = F/m).

🌍 Real-World Examples
Example 1: Walking

Action: Your foot pushes backward on the ground.
Reaction: The ground pushes forward on your foot.
Result: You move forward. Without this reaction force, you couldn't walk!

Example 2: Swimming

Action: A swimmer pushes water backward with their arms and legs.
Reaction: Water pushes the swimmer forward.
Result: The swimmer moves through the water.

Example 3: Rocket Launch

Action: Rocket expels hot gases downward with great force.
Reaction: Gases push the rocket upward with equal force.
Result: The rocket accelerates upward into space. (This is Newton's Third Law in action!)

Example 4: Jumping

Action: Your legs push down on the ground with force.
Reaction: The ground pushes up on you with equal force.
Result: You launch into the air. The stronger you push down, the higher you jump!

Example 5: Collision

Action: Car A hits Car B with 5000 N of force to the right.
Reaction: Car B hits Car A with 5000 N of force to the left.
Result: Both cars feel equal impact forces. Their accelerations differ based on their masses.

∑ Mathematical Expression
F₁ = -F₂
Force on object 1 equals negative force on object 2

What this means:

  • F₁ = force exerted BY object 1 ON object 2
  • F₂ = force exerted BY object 2 ON object 1
  • Negative sign (-) = opposite direction
  • Magnitude: |F₁| = |F₂| (same size)
Mathematical Example:

If object A pushes object B with a force of +100 N (to the right),
then object B pushes object A with a force of -100 N (to the left).
F₁ = -F₂ → 100 = -(-100) ✓

🤔 Why Do Objects Move Differently?

Even though action and reaction forces are equal, objects may accelerate differently. This is because acceleration depends on MASS.

a = F / m
Why a Person Can't Push a Wall

The forces are equal:
• You push wall: 100 N to the right
• Wall pushes you: 100 N to the left

But accelerations differ:
• Your mass: ~70 kg → a = 100 ÷ 70 = 1.43 m/s² (you move!)
• Wall's mass: ~1,000,000 kg → a = 100 ÷ 1,000,000 = 0.0001 m/s² (barely moves!)

Key insight: The wall exerts just as much force on you as you exert on it! You move backward because you have less mass, not because the force on you is different.

✅ Learning Checklist
🎓 Key Takeaways
Remember These Points:
  • F₁ = -F₂: Action and reaction forces are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction
  • Different objects: Each force acts on a different object (crucial!)
  • Same type: Both forces are the same type (both contact, both gravity, etc.)
  • No cancellation: They don't cancel because they're on different objects
  • Always occur together: One cannot exist without the other
  • Mass matters: Different accelerations result from different masses, not different forces
  • Universal principle: Applies to ALL forces: pushes, pulls, friction, gravity, etc.
