📖 Definition & Core Concept
What is Newton's Second Law?

Newton's Second Law of Motion is the most important law of motion. It explains the relationship between force, mass, and acceleration.

F = m × a
Force equals mass times acceleration

This law tells us that acceleration is directly proportional to the applied force and inversely proportional to the mass of the object. In simpler terms: the more force you apply to an object, the faster it accelerates; the heavier the object, the slower it accelerates (for the same force).

∑ Understanding the Formula
Formula: F = m × a
Variables Explained:
  • F = Net Force (Newtons, N) = kg·m/s²
  • m = Mass (kilograms, kg)
  • a = Acceleration (meters per second squared, m/s²)
Example: Pushing a Cart

If you push a 10 kg cart with a force of 50 N, the acceleration will be:
a = F ÷ m = 50 N ÷ 10 kg = 5 m/s²

The cart accelerates at 5 meters per second squared.

Alternative Forms:
a = F / m
Acceleration equals force divided by mass
m = F / a
Mass equals force divided by acceleration
🔗 Key Relationships
Direct Proportionality: Force & Acceleration

If you double the force, you double the acceleration (keeping mass constant).

Example:

• Force = 10 N, Mass = 2 kg → Acceleration = 5 m/s²
• Force = 20 N, Mass = 2 kg → Acceleration = 10 m/s²
→ Doubling force doubles acceleration

Inverse Proportionality: Mass & Acceleration

If you double the mass, you halve the acceleration (keeping force constant).

Example:

• Force = 20 N, Mass = 2 kg → Acceleration = 10 m/s²
• Force = 20 N, Mass = 4 kg → Acceleration = 5 m/s²
→ Doubling mass halves acceleration

🌍 Real-World Applications
Example 1: Accelerating a Car

A 1000 kg car with an engine producing 5000 N of force will accelerate at:
a = 5000 N ÷ 1000 kg = 5 m/s²
A heavier car (2000 kg) with the same engine will only accelerate at 2.5 m/s².

Example 2: Throwing a Ball vs. Throwing a Rock

If you apply the same force throwing a light ball and a heavy rock, the ball accelerates faster because it has less mass.

Example 3: Rocket Launch

Rockets produce massive thrust (force). Initially, with full fuel load (large mass), acceleration is moderate. As fuel burns, mass decreases, so the same thrust produces greater acceleration.

Example 4: Impact Force in Collisions

A heavy truck (large mass) traveling at high speed requires enormous force to stop quickly, which is why high-speed truck collisions are so destructive.

Example 5: Skateboard Physics

Pushing a light skateboard produces quick acceleration. Adding weight (a person) to the skateboard increases mass, requiring more force for the same acceleration.

⚙️ Units & Conversions
Newton: Unit of Force

One Newton (N) is the amount of force needed to accelerate a 1 kg object at 1 m/s².

1 N = 1 kg·m/s²
Common Conversions:
  • 1 Newton ≈ 0.225 pounds-force (lbf)
  • 1 pound-force ≈ 4.45 Newtons
  • 1 kg ≈ 9.8 N of weight force (on Earth)

🔍 Problem-Solving Steps
How to Solve F = ma Problems:
1
Identify what you know: List the given values (F, m, or a)
2
Identify what you need to find: Which variable are you solving for?
3
Choose the right formula: F = ma, a = F/m, or m = F/a
4
Substitute the values: Plug in numbers with units
5
Calculate and check units: Ensure answer has correct units
Complete Example:

Problem: A 5 kg object is pushed with a force of 20 N. What is its acceleration?

Step 1-2: Given: m = 5 kg, F = 20 N; Find: a
Step 3: Use: a = F/m
Step 4: a = 20 N ÷ 5 kg
Step 5: a = 4 m/s² ✓

🎓 Key Takeaways
Remember These Points:
  • F = m × a is the most important equation in mechanics
  • Force causes acceleration: More force → more acceleration
  • Mass resists acceleration: More mass → less acceleration
  • Newton (N): Unit of force = kg·m/s²
  • Direct proportion: F and a increase/decrease together
  • Inverse proportion: m and a increase/decrease oppositely
  • All three variables matter: You need to know the relationships between them
