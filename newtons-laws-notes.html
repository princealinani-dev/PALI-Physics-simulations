1st Law of Motion: Law of Inertia

Statement: An object at rest remains at rest, and an object in motion remains in motion at a constant velocity unless acted upon by an external force.

ΣF = 0 ⟹ a = 0 (No acceleration)

Understanding the Concept

Newton's First Law describes the concept of Inertia, which is the natural tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. This law forms the foundation of classical mechanics and explains why objects behave the way they do without external forces.

Key Points

  • Inertia depends on mass - more massive objects have greater inertia
  • An object in motion will never stop by itself without a force
  • This explains why we need seatbelts - our bodies want to keep moving when a car stops
  • In space, objects continue moving indefinitely without forces

Real-World Examples

🚗 Car Stopping: When you suddenly brake, your body continues moving forward due to inertia (hence the seatbelt!).

📖 Book on Table: A book resting on a table stays there until you push it. The table exerts an upward force (normal force) that balances gravity.

🌍 Planets: Planets orbit the sun because gravity provides the force. Without it, they'd travel in straight lines.

2nd Law of Motion: F = ma

Statement: The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force applied and inversely proportional to its mass.

F = ma
Where:
F = Net Force (Newtons, N)
m = Mass (kilograms, kg)
a = Acceleration (m/s²)

Understanding the Concept

The Second Law quantifies the relationship between force, mass, and acceleration. It shows that:

  • Larger forces produce larger accelerations
  • More mass requires more force for the same acceleration
  • This is the most practical law for solving physics problems

Key Points

  • Direct Proportionality: Double the force → double the acceleration
  • Inverse Proportionality: Double the mass → half the acceleration
  • The net force is what matters, not individual forces
  • Force and acceleration always point in the same direction

Problem Example

Question: A 2 kg object experiences a net force of 10 N. What is its acceleration?

Solution: F = ma → 10 = 2 × a → a = 5 m/s²

Answer: The object accelerates at 5 meters per second squared.

3rd Law of Motion: Action-Reaction

Statement: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. If object A exerts a force on object B, then object B exerts an equal force in the opposite direction on object A.

F_A on B = -F_B on A
(Forces are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction)

Understanding the Concept

The Third Law states that forces always come in pairs. This law explains how objects interact with each other through forces. It's crucial to understand that action-reaction forces:

  • Act on different objects
  • Are equal in magnitude
  • Are opposite in direction
  • Occur simultaneously

Key Points

  • Never cancels out: Action-reaction forces act on different objects
  • You push on a wall, the wall pushes back on you with equal force
  • A rocket pushes gas downward, gas pushes rocket upward
  • Your feet push Earth backward, Earth pushes you forward (you jump)

Real-World Examples

🚀 Rocket Launch: Rocket expels hot gases downward (action). The gases push the rocket upward (reaction).

🏊 Swimming: You push water backward (action). Water pushes you forward (reaction).

🪘 Drumming: Your hand pushes the drum downward (action). The drum pushes your hand upward (reaction).

🌍 Walking: Your foot pushes Earth backward (action). Earth pushes your foot forward (reaction).

Common Misconception

❌ Wrong: Action and reaction forces cancel out to produce no net force.

✓ Correct: Action and reaction forces act on different objects, so they don't cancel. Each object experiences a different net force.
